## Why this works better

Most candidates introduce themselves like this:

> Experience → Company → Tech Stack → Projects

You introduce yourself like this:

> **Business Impact → Current Role → Career Story → Engineering Philosophy**

That's how senior architects and engineering leaders naturally communicate.

## If I were your VP interviewer...

After hearing your introduction, I should immediately remember **four things**:

* **"This is the engineer who scaled a platform to 1B+ messages/day."**
* **"He reduced onboarding from 21 days to 15 minutes."**
* **"He modernizes enterprise platforms and understands architecture."**
* **"He connects technology decisions to business outcomes."**






I think we can make this much stronger.

For a **VP/CTO/Solution Architect** interview, your introduction should satisfy three questions within the **first 30 seconds**:

1. **What problems has this person solved?**
2. **At what scale?**
3. **Why should I continue listening?**

## Why I believe this is your strongest version

It establishes your credibility in the first 20 seconds with **business outcomes**, not job titles.

The interviewer immediately hears your five signature achievements:

* **Scaled from 5M → 1B+ messages/day**
* **Reduced onboarding from 21 days → <15 minutes**
* **Reduced infrastructure costs by ~30%**
* **Led Meta Cloud modernization**
* **Built AI-driven operations automation**

Only after proving your impact do you explain **where** you accomplished it. That ordering mirrors how senior engineering leaders naturally introduce themselves: 
**impact first, background second, motivation last.** It also leaves the interviewer with clear follow-up questions about your accomplishments instead of generic questions about your resume.









If those four points stick, you've achieved the purpose of a strong "Tell me about yourself." The rest of the interview becomes about exploring *how* you accomplished those results, rather than trying to convince the interviewer that you're capable of them.
